The Flawed Vision: The "Radiant Garden City"

Jane Jacobs critiqued mid-20th-century urban planning for its rigid, top-down approach. This model, which she termed the "Radiant Garden City," was based on segregating uses and creating sterile, monotonous super-blocks. This approach, she argued, resulted in lifeless, unsafe, and ultimately failed urban spaces.

The Four Pillars of a Living City

1. Mixed Primary Uses

A healthy district must combine different primary functions like residences, workplaces, and cultural institutions. This ensures a continuous flow of people with different schedules, creating constant, natural surveillance and economic symbiosis.

2. Short Blocks

Frequent streets and opportunities to turn corners are essential. They create permeable, walkable neighborhoods, increase economic cross-use, and foster more casual public encounters, which are the bedrock of a safe and sociable community.

3. Aged Buildings

A mix of building ages is crucial for economic diversity. Old, low-rent buildings are incubators for new ideas, small businesses, and startups that cannot afford the high overhead of new construction. This mix creates both economic and visual richness.

4. Dense Concentration

Cities need a dense concentration of people to support a wide variety of enterprises and amenities. High density, when combined with the other three conditions, creates the vitality, excitement, and economic opportunities that are the hallmark of a successful city.

The Heart of the City: The Sidewalk Ballet

For Jacobs, the sidewalk is the city's most vital organ. It's where the intricate "ballet" of public life unfolds. This constant, casual interaction between residents, shopkeepers, and strangers is what makes a street safe, sociable, and alive. The key is the principle of "Eyes on the Street."

Case Study: A Jacobsian City in the Canadian Wilderness

If building a city from scratch, Jacobs would argue against suburban sprawl that devours nature. Instead, she would advocate for a compact, dense urban core that preserves the surrounding wilderness by creating a sharp, clear border between city and nature.
