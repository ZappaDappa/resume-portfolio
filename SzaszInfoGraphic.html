The Fundamental Error

Mind is a Verb.

Szasz argues our greatest mistake is treating "the mind" as a noun‚Äîa thing or an object like the brain. Instead, "to mind" is an activity. It's what we do when we pay attention, communicate, take responsibility, and create meaning through language.

The Process of Minding: From Sensation to Personhood

The ability "to mind" is not innate; it is learned. Szasz uses the story of Helen Keller to show how language transforms a being that only senses into a person that thinks, communicates, and reflects. This process is built on two types of dialogue.

1. Interlocution (Dialogue with Others)

The foundation of learning. We learn language by communicating with other people.

2. Autologue (Dialogue with Oneself)

This is what we call "thinking." It is the uniquely human ability to speak silently to ourselves, which allows for self-awareness, planning, and morality.

Result: A Moral Agent

Through these dialogues, a person emerges‚Äîsomeone who is aware, responsible, and can give an account of their actions.

What Makes a Person?

According to Szasz, personhood is not a mixture of biology and mentality. It is a singular concept rooted in moral agency and the ability to communicate. It cannot be reduced to brain chemistry.

Psychiatry and the Erosion of Responsibility

Szasz argues that by framing misbehavior as "mental illness"‚Äîa brain disease‚Äîwe strip individuals of their personhood. This medicalizes moral problems and turns responsibility into a clinical diagnosis. He contrasts legitimate brain diseases with behaviors labeled as illnesses.

Memory: A Recording or a Story?

Modern psychology often treats memory as a biological hard drive where experiences are stored and can be "recovered." Szasz rejects this, arguing that memory is a creative, moral act of storytelling that serves our present needs.

The "Hard Drive" Myth üíæ

Views memory as a passive recording of the past. This leads to dangerous ideas like "False Memory Syndrome," where therapists claim to "recover" memories that never existed.

The "Narrative" Reality üìñ

Views remembering as an active process of constructing a story about our past to make sense of our present and justify our future. It is a personal and moral activity.
