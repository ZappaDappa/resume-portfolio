Reclaiming Stirner: A Necessary Retranslation

For over a century, the English-speaking world knew Stirner's work as "The Ego and His Own." The 1907 translation by Steven T. Byington framed the work in abstract psychological terms, missing the author's concrete, aggressive, and playful style. Wolfi Landstreicher's 2017 translation, "The Unique and Its Property," restores the original's confrontational spirit and philosophical precision.

Byington (1907) - The Ego and His Own

  • Frames the individual as an abstract "Ego."
  • Softens the aggressive, provocative tone for a Victorian audience.
  • Imposes a systematic structure on an unsystematic work.
  • Misses the humor and "playful ferocity."

Landstreicher (2017) - The Unique and Its Property

  • Restores "The Unique," the concrete, living individual.
  • Captures the original crude, humorous, and biting style.
  • Respects the work as a personal provocation, not a formal system.
  • Highlights Stirner's critique of all abstractions.

Ownness vs. Freedom: Reclaiming Sovereignty

Stirner rejects the common idea of "freedom," seeing it as a conditional gift from a higher power (like a state granting rights). He proposes "Ownness" as the true form of liberation: the absolute, internal sovereignty of the individual over their own mind and body, independent of any external authority.

Models of Association: The State vs. The Union of Egoists

Stirner critiques compulsory social structures like the State and Society, which demand the individual's submission. He contrasts this with the "Union of Egoists," a voluntary, temporary, and tool-like association created by individuals purely for their mutual benefit, which can be dissolved at any time.
